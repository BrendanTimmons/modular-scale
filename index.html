Modular Scale

By Brendan Timmons ― 30th of July, 2017.

When starting a design there are many considerations. Typography being the cornerstone of any good design can have huge effects on the overall feel. Choosing the typeface is only one step in the process. The sizes at which one composes their type can change the mood or tone completely.

A modular scale, like a musical scale, is a prearranged set of harmonious proportions. ― The Elements of Typographic Style, Robert Bringhurst 1992

A change in moving type

In 1494 a man named Aldus Manutius founded the Aldine Press in Venice with the intention of publishing classic Greek literature. At this point in time printed books were only around 50 years old and were published primarily by the church in blackletter.

It was Aldus who triggered a change from large books printed in blackletter to smaller books with Roman type which was more economical and easier to read than the books published by the church. Aldus also produced the first italic font along with a matching greek typeface.

Aldus published his first book Cardinal Pietro Bembo’s De Aetna in 1495. However, most renowned work by the Aldine Press is Francesco Colonna’s Hypnerotomachia Poliphili, published in 1499. Still referenced today for the way it matched text and illustration in terms of it’s typographic colour. Archive.org has a digital version of the original book.

Modular scales are evident Aldus’s work. From everything in the size and spacing of type, to even sub-modular levels […] and in whole page layouts. ― More Meaningful Typography, Tim Brown 2010

During the sixteenth century, European typographers devised a set of common sizes which remained nearly unchanged for 400 years. This scale became known as the classic typographic scale with a scaling ratio of two. Classic musical scales also share the same ratio where on a piano each C note vibrates twice as fast as the prior C note.

What defines a scale

A scale is made up by three things: base size, ratio, and number of steps. The base is multiplied or divided by the ratio which results in a series of harmonious steps.

There are many pre-existing scales that have been used over the centuries. Which one to use―or creating your own―is something a typographer must consider when beginning to compose. There are numerous scales some based on mathematical formulas such as φ (1:1618), π (1:3.141) or based on musical scales to name a few: the classic scale (1:2), major third (4:5), augmented fourth (1:√2).

Continuing with the classic scale for example, selecting a base of 18 pixels yields the following:

You can continue further up or down the scale for larger or smaller sizes that still maintain the same rhythm.

Selecting a base

Once you have a typeface selected for your content you must establish a base. All typefaces are not created equal however, 16px on one does not make it’s size identical to another. Differing Em boxes on typefaces will change how the fonts are rendered at certain sizes. With this in mind experimenting with the size of your chosen font at differing sizes to find at which size it really shines.

The body copy of this article uses Spectral from google fonts. Closely inspecting the sizes below, at 18px the arm on the T is blurry and at 16px the serifs on the Z are also blurry. 17px was the clear winner with the best looking size for rendering body copy with 15px being too small.

Time to get huge

Now that we’ve defined our base we can move onto our ratio. The choices made here are largely up to what kind of content or the overall feel. If you know that your titles are short and need impact then using ratios such as major twelfth (1:3) π (1:3141) or double octave (1:4) will create very large differences between your headings and body copy.

I’ve found that a variety of things can serve as an important number. The size at which caption text looks best […] or a large number like the width of a piece of media […] ensures that something about those elements resonates with the layout as a whole. ― More Meaningful Typography, Tim Brown 2010

For this article augmented fourth also known as dim fifth (1:√2) has been used for obvious reasons…

The need for more

Sometimes the scale steps are too large and it would be useful to have something between 72px and 144px. This is where multi-stranded modular scales come in handy by introducing additional steps based on one or more additional base sizes.

Chosing a second base shouldn’t be done at random. Finding something such as a citation, caption, even a video or image block that requires a fixed size can be a deciding factor. In this article I decided that I would like my blockquote citations to render at around 14px. This article uses 1.0625em sized for body copy and 1.238em sized for citations.
